# Global Family-Friendly Cycling Standards

Academic and government frameworks that define what counts as safe and comfortable cycling infrastructure for families with young children. This is the evidence base for **Layer 1** of family-bike-map's scoring model.

## TL;DR

There is **no single ISO-style global standard**, but every major framework converges on the same two variables — **motor-vehicle speed × volume** — with breakpoints within ~20% of each other. Two frameworks explicitly target families:

- **Level of Traffic Stress (LTS 1–4)** — LTS 1 is *defined* as "suitable for children" (Mekuria, Furth & Nixon, Mineta 2012).
- **NACTO "Designing for All Ages & Abilities" (AAA)** — designed for "an 8- to 80-year-old."

Both calibrate against the Dutch **CROW Design Manual**. The UK's **LTN 1/20** uses literally the same five design principles as CROW (Coherent, Direct, Safe, Comfortable, Attractive) and explicitly names "a 12-year-old child cycling to school" as the design user.

## Academic research

### Parental perception of cycling safety with children

- Frater et al., [**Critical Environmental Factors for Transportation Cycling in Children** (PLOS ONE, 2014)](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0106696) — bike-along interviews with children; key factors are traffic volume/speed, separation, intersections, surface quality.
- Carver et al., [**Attitudes to walking and cycling among children, young people and parents: a systematic review** (PubMed, 2008)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18791040/).
- Rothman et al., [**Objective and Perceived Traffic Safety for Children** (IJERPH, 2022)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8910047/) — systematic review.
- Macpherson et al., [**Public health approaches to safer cycling for children based on developmental and physiological readiness** (2018)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5887771/) — explicitly discusses ages 3–8 cognitive limits (attention, working memory, hazard anticipation).
- [**Improving cycling safety for children and youth** (Paediatrics & Child Health, Oxford, 2024)](https://academic.oup.com/pch/article/29/5/324/7756834).

### Level of Traffic Stress (LTS)

- Mekuria, Furth & Nixon, [**Low-Stress Bicycling and Network Connectivity** (Mineta Transportation Institute 11-19, 2012, PDF)](https://transweb.sjsu.edu/sites/default/files/1005-low-stress-bicycling-network-connectivity.pdf) — origin paper. LTS 1 explicitly defined as "suitable for children."
- Furth, Mekuria & Nixon, [**Network Connectivity for Low-Stress Bicycling** (TRR 2016)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3141/2587-06).
- Peter Furth's canonical LTS criteria page: <https://peterfurth.sites.northeastern.edu/level-of-traffic-stress/>
- [Boston Bicycle LTS Technical Documentation (2020, PDF)](https://www.boston.gov/sites/default/files/file/2020/12/Bicycle%20Level%20of%20Traffic%20Stress%20Report%20&%20Guide%20for%20Large%20Developments.pdf) — a concrete municipal LTS implementation.

LTS tiers:
- **LTS 1** — children / "most children" (the family-bike-map target)
- **LTS 2** — "most adults including interested but concerned" (calibrated against Dutch CROW)
- **LTS 3** — enthused & confident
- **LTS 4** — strong & fearless

## Government / design-manual standards

### Netherlands — CROW Design Manual for Bicycle Traffic

Built on **Sustainable Safety**. The **Five Main Requirements**: Cohesion, Directness, Safety, Comfort, Attractiveness.

- [CROW Design Manual — Wikipedia overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CROW_Design_Manual_for_Bicycle_Traffic)
- [CROW Platform product page](https://crowplatform.com/product/design-manual-for-bicycle-traffic/)
- [FHWA — The Dutch Approach to Bicycle Mobility](https://international.fhwa.dot.gov/pubs/pl18004/chap03.cfm)
- [Dutch Cycling Embassy — Five Design Principles](https://dutchcycling.nl/knowledge/knowledge-clips/five-design-principles-for-bicycle-infrastructure/)

Separation rules derive from motor-traffic speed/volume: cycle traffic mixes with motor traffic only on access streets ≤30 km/h with low volumes (typically <2,000–2,500 motor vehicles/day). Above that, separated cycle tracks are required. On 50 km/h distributor roads, fully separated cycle tracks are mandatory.

### Denmark — Vejdirektoratet / Copenhagen

- [Vejdirektoratet — Cycling and Safety for Cyclists, Danish Experiences (PDF)](https://www.vejdirektoratet.dk/sites/default/files/publications/cycling_and_safety_for_cyclist.pdf)
- [Danish Road Directorate — Collection of Cycle Concepts (NACTO mirror)](https://nacto.org/wp-content/uploads/Road-Directorate-Collection-Cycle-Concepts-2000.pdf)
- [Copenhagen — Cycle-Friendly Infrastructure 2024 (PDF)](https://kk.sites.itera.dk/apps/kk_pub2/pdf/2947_ac6e083026df.pdf)
- [Cycling Solutions — KK / Vejdirektoratet best practice](https://cyclingsolutions.info/infrastructure-road-stretches-and-intersections/)

**Hard threshold:** separated cycle tracks required when motor traffic exceeds ~2,500 vehicles/day OR speed limit >40 km/h. Hierarchy: (1) car-free paths, (2) one-way curb-separated cycle tracks (the Danish default on distributors), (3) painted cycle lanes (only acceptable below those thresholds), (4) mixed traffic (only on quiet 30 km/h streets). Copenhagen's stated target is "comfortable for an 8-year-old."

### Germany — ERA (Empfehlungen für Radverkehrsanlagen) + StVO

- [Empfehlungen für Radverkehrsanlagen — Wikipedia (DE)](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empfehlungen_f%C3%BCr_Radverkehrsanlagen)
- [stvo2go — Fahrradstraße legal requirements](https://www.stvo2go.de/fahrradstrasse-einrichten/)

ERA uses a load-class diagram (motor traffic volume × speed) to pick the facility. Shared traffic acceptable only at low speeds and <~2,500 Kfz/day; protected cycle tracks required above ~10,000 Kfz/day or 50 km/h.

**Fahrradstraße (StVO §45 / Zeichen 244):** legal definition — bicycles have priority, max 30 km/h for any permitted motor traffic, established only where bike traffic is or will be the dominant mode, low parking pressure, no through motor traffic.

Berlin's Mobilitätsgesetz (2018) §43 explicitly requires infrastructure usable by children and seniors.

### USA — NACTO + FHWA + LTS

- [NACTO — Designing for All Ages and Abilities (web)](https://nacto.org/publication/urban-bikeway-design-guide/planning-and-developing-a-bikeable-city/designing-for-all-ages-and-abilities/)
- [NACTO — AAA PDF (2017)](https://nacto.org/wp-content/uploads/NACTO_Designing-for-All-Ages-Abilities.pdf)
- [NACTO — Design Controls for Bicycle Facilities](https://nacto.org/publication/urban-bikeway-design-guide/designing-bikeways-for-all-ages-and-abilities/design-controls-for-bicycle-facilities/)
- [NACTO — Bike Boulevards](https://nacto.org/publication/urban-bikeway-design-guide/designing-bikeways-for-all-ages-and-abilities/bikeways-on-low-speed-low-volume-streets/bike-boulevards/)
- [FHWA Bikeway Selection Guide (PDF)](https://safety.fhwa.dot.gov/ped_bike/tools_solve/docs/fhwasa18077.pdf)

NACTO AAA concrete thresholds:
- **Mixed traffic / bike boulevard**: ≤3,000 veh/day and 85th-percentile speed ≤25 mph (40 km/h)
- **Buffered bike lane (AAA)**: up to 6,000 veh/day, ≤25 mph
- **Constrained bike lane**: <8,000 veh/day, <25 mph
- **Protected bike lane required** above 6,000 veh/day, >25 mph, multi-lane, or high curbside demand

FHWA: dedicated lane >2,000 ADT or >20 mph; fully separated >6,000 ADT and 30 mph.

### UK — LTN 1/20

- [GOV.UK — LTN 1/20 Cycle Infrastructure Design](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/cycle-infrastructure-design-ltn-120)
- [LTN 1/20 full document (PDF)](https://nsip-documents.planninginspectorate.gov.uk/published-documents/TR010038-000597-LTN-1-20-cycle-infrastructure-design-4816-1.pdf)

Five **Core Design Principles**: Coherent, Direct, Safe, Comfortable, Attractive (identical wording to CROW). **Table 4-1** gives a speed/volume matrix: protection threshold 2,500 PCU/day at 30 mph; separation recommended for any street >2,000 PCU/day; mixed traffic only at ≤20 mph with low flows.

LTN 1/20 is the only manual of the five that explicitly cites "a 12-year-old child cycling to school" as the design user.

## Convergent universal backbone

Every framework reduces to **(motor vehicle speed) × (motor vehicle volume)** with breakpoints within ~20%:

| Tier | Speed | Volume (veh/day) | Required facility |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Great** | car-free | 0 | Separated path / Fahrradstraße / bike boulevard |
| **Good** | ≤30 km/h | <2,000–2,500 | Mixed traffic acceptable |
| **OK** | ≤30–40 km/h | 2,000–6,000 | Cycle lane with buffer / light segregation |
| **Required separation** | >40 km/h OR >6,000 | — | Curb-separated cycle track |
| **Avoid** | >50 km/h unprotected | any | Not AAA / LTS 4 |

## The 3–5 most important variables

Across LTS, NACTO AAA, CROW, ERA, and the parent-perception literature, the same variables recur:

1. **Physical separation from motor traffic** — single biggest predictor. Protected/cycle track = LTS 1 regardless of road class. NACTO requires protection above 25 mph / 40 km/h or >6,000 vpd.
2. **Motor-vehicle speed** (posted + 85th percentile) — NACTO mixed-traffic threshold is ≤20 mph / 30 km/h; LTS 1 mixed traffic requires very low speeds.
3. **Motor-vehicle volume (AADT)** — LTS 1 mixed traffic requires ≤1,500–3,000 vpd on a 2-lane road with no centerline; NACTO AAA caps shared streets at ~1,500 vpd.
4. **Intersection / crossing stress** — unsignalized crossings of multilane or fast roads dominate the overall route LTS (the route inherits its worst link). Signal protection, refuge islands, and crossing distance matter.
5. **Through lanes & on-street parking interactions** ("door zone," blockage frequency).

Secondary but research-supported for ages 3–8: **surface quality** (children fall easily on cobbles/gravel), **gradient/distance** (stamina caps a 5-year-old at ~3–5 km continuous), **wayfinding simplicity** (cognitive load — kids cannot process complex junctions until ~age 10), **absence of heavy goods vehicles**.

## Gap analysis vs. our current model

Our 5-tier model (Great / Good / OK / Acceptable / Avoid) maps almost 1:1 onto LTS 1 → LTS 4. The main gaps for global portability:

1. **Score intersections separately** using LTS weakest-link rule. Unsignalized crossings of fast multilane roads should dominate a route's score — we do not currently model this.
2. **Key classification off `(maxspeed, AADT)` not `highway=` class.** The same `highway=tertiary` means very different things in Berlin vs. Houston.
3. **Add gradient penalty** for kid stamina (already partially addressed by the 3 km/h toddler walking-speed change, not on cycling side).
4. **Add surface penalty** (cobbles/sett) — Berlin-specific but will matter in any European historic center.
